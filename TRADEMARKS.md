# OpenLeague Trademark Policy

The **OpenLeague source code** is free and open source under the
[Apache License 2.0](./LICENSE). You may fork it, modify it, self-host it, and
use it commercially — freely, at no charge, with no permission required.

The **name "OpenLeague" and the OpenLeague logo** are different. They are
trademarks of Mark Beacom, and they are **not** covered by the Apache 2.0
license. The code is yours to use; the brand is not. This is the same model
used by open-source projects like Ghost and Mattermost: open code, protected
name.

The point is simple. When someone sees the OpenLeague name or logo, they should
be able to trust that it refers to this project. That trust only works if the
marks aren't used in ways that confuse or mislead people.

## What you can do without asking

You are welcome to make honest, factual references to OpenLeague — no
permission needed:

- Say your project is **"compatible with OpenLeague"** or **"built on OpenLeague."**
- Describe your work as **"a fork of OpenLeague"** or **"based on OpenLeague."**
- Use the name in blog posts, tutorials, talks, and documentation that discuss
  the project accurately.

## What needs written permission

Please **do not** do any of the following without written permission from
Mark Beacom:

1. **Name your fork or distribution "OpenLeague"** — or any name confusingly
   similar to it. If you distribute a modified version, give it your own name.
2. **Use the OpenLeague name or logo to market or sell a competing hosted or
   SaaS service.** The code license lets you run a competing service; the
   trademark does not let you brand it as OpenLeague.
3. **Use the marks in any way that implies endorsement, sponsorship, or
   official affiliation** with OpenLeague or Mark Beacom when none exists.

## If you fork

We encourage forks — that's what open source is for. When you do, please
**choose your own name and your own logo.** It keeps things clear for users of
both projects, and it's the neighborly thing to do.

## Questions

Not sure whether your use is okay? Just ask. We're happy to grant permission
for reasonable uses, and we'd rather have a quick conversation than a
misunderstanding.
